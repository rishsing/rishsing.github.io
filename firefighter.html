Firefighter Tool Design

Re-thinking Mobile Data Terminals


Interaction Design, Nov 2015
(3 weeks)

Backdrop

In this project, we were presented with a real scenario where new technology was being introduced to firefighters in Bloomington, Indiana. Called resc.info, the technology is designed to deliver the right information that firefighters need at different points in a response. But exactly how to deliver that information, on what devices and in what form was upto us to design.

User Research

How we did it ?

For purposes of our research, we interviewed David Wild (Associate Professor @ Indiana University), Jon Musgrave (Captain @ Station 3) and other firefighters at University Station, Bloomington, Indiana. We had prepared a good list of questions to ask them to understand how they respond to an emergency. To get a more clear picture, I also conducted a contextual inquiry session wherein I went on a ride along with a fireifgher and observed his actions as he responded to an emergency.



Insights

What we found ?

Firefighters use Mobile Data Terminals


Firefighters use Mobile Data Terminals, a robust laptop mounted on the dashboard of their trucks when they are enroute to a scene. The system is still new to them and they are not able to use it to its fullest capacity owing to its cluttered inteface.

Firefighters need specific information


Firefighters want to see the history of emergencies that took place in the building they are responding to, any hazardous materials that might be present in that building and the building floor plan.

Firefighter use MDT while wearing gloves


Firefighters use Mobile Data Terminals while they are wearing gloves during a fast, bumpy ride to the scene they are responding to. Consequently, using the trackpad and operating the MDT becomes all the more difficult.



MDT system does not give the firefighters the right information when they need it and is difficult to operate during the ride.


Also, given the budget constraints that the fire department had, we decided to constraint ourselves to designing for laptops that firefighters already have in their trucks. We designed for the time when they are driving to the scene because thats when they are using the system. Once they arrive on scene, their instinct takes over.

So for our solution, we focused on:

Re-designing the MDTs (Mobile Data Terminals) to make it easy to use and also to deliver the right information to the firefighters when they need it.

Our Concept



We wanted to create a simple interface which would not only give firefighters the information they need but would also be easy to use during a fast, bumpy ride. Therefore, for starters, we decided that its important to include information regarding:

  • Building Plan
  • History of emergencies(that took place in that building)
  • Hydrant Location
  • Hazard Information


No two fire calls are the same and firefighters must adjust their planning for each call. Each adjustment requires viewing of different set of information. Therefore, we decided to make the information thats displayed on the screen customizable . In order to do that, we decided to have toggle buttons to toggle on and off the information they see from each of the four categories.


To make MDTs more easy to operate with gloves during a fast, bumpy ride, we decided to break the keyboard into four quadrants wherein each quadrant corresponds to one of the four categories. Pressing keys in any quadrant will toggle on and off the information from that corresponding category.


Prototype





FIRST SCREEN

This is the first screen the firefighter sees when he/she enters the truck. On the top right corner the screen is the address of the place where they need to go. On the top-left corner of the screen is the dispatch call. All additional information coming from dispatch will be displayed there. To view the information from any of the category above, the firefighter will have to press keys in the corresponding quadrant.




For instance, pressing keys in the lower right corner of the keyboard will bring up information from 'Hazards' category. The information will mainly be about hazardous materials present in and around the location the fireifghters are responding to.









RESPONSIVE INFORMATION SILOS

We made the information silos/layout responsive so that firefighters can see multiple categories at once. No two calls are the same and every call requires an adjustment in their planning. This adjustment, in turn, warrants viewing different sets of information for better planning & preparedness. Therefore, as an example, pressing keys on the top-left corner of the keyboard will bring up information from the 'History' category and the layout will be adjusted to fit in two different sets of information.


Team Process



What I learned ?

  1. Designing in context is very important. A product is successful only if it can be used by its intended users easily and effectively. Almost every product is used in some context or the other. The Mobile Data Terminal is used by firefighters when they are wearing their heavy gear are enroute to a scene. This essentially means using a laptop with thick, heavy gloves during a speedy and bumpy ride. Its easy to see how that made using the MDT difficult. So if we were to design anything that the firefighters could use, we had to recognize the context in which they would use our product. This tremendously impacted the usability of our product and guided our thought process.

Tools used:      

Uber Rating Re-design

Asking good questions !

  • What information do you get from dispatch and in what form ?
  • What do you do after you get a call from dispatch ? Why do you follow that process ?
  • What information do you exactly need regarding, say a building fire and how does that help you handle the emergency?
  • How comfortable are you with technology ? What role does technology play in day to day operations ? Do you view technology as a source of information only or can it contribute more to your day to day information ?
  • Do you, as a firefighter, want information specific to your role ?
  • What is the most basic information that you need that you absolutely cannot work without ?
  • What does a typical dispatch message look like ?
